What a China Railway Stock Taught Me About the A-share Market

By Wildsheepchase, 2015 06 04 Thursday, in category 2015

Capital Market

For the last year or so, I have spent a lot more time investing in the A-share market. For someone who has been mainly investing in Chinese companies listed in the US and Hong Kong, entering the A-share market is like sailing from calm water into storms. This post is not a discussion about whether the A-share market is in a bubble or when the bubble is going to burst. Rather, I'm going to share what I have learned about the intricacies of this market.

Here is the synoposis of my roller-coaster ride with the stock.

I first bought CSR Corp (A-share) in November and December 2013 at prices ranging between 5.19 and 5.48 Yuan per share. More was bought in late July 2014 at 5.19 Yuan per share, but it was still not a major position in the portfolio. In Octover, I did more work on CSR and decided to make it a bigger bet because:

  1. The company is a solid company that manufactures products that I have been using intensively (riding the high speed rails back and forth between Beijing and Shanghai),
  2. The prospects for the company to export rail products is promising technologically and politically, and
  3. The stock, at that time, was trading at mid-teens trailing p/e multiples.

Just as I was happily prepared to increase the bet, the company announced a trading halt due to "major restructuring" on October 27, 2014. When it was announced, nobody knew the halt will last for two whole months. A merger with CNR was announced in December, creating a monopoly of rail equipment manufacturing in China, and the stock resumed trading on December 31, 2014. To my dismay the only thing I could do was to watch the stock price surged from 5.81 to 10.27 within six trading days without being able to buy any at "market" prices.

Here's is why. A-share market has a 10% price change limit rule, i.e., the price movement in a single day cannot exceed 10% of the previous day's closing price, either up or down. I don't know for sure why this rule came into being, although I suspect it's the securities regulator playing its paternalistic protective role. This arbitrary limit of market volatility is a big disruption to market continuity and liquidity. In the case of CSR, even though I was willing to pay 7 Yuan for the stock, the stock stopped trading once it hits 6.39 (10% increase from the previous closing price) on December 31. CSR hit 10% up limit for six consecutive trading days until the price reached 10.27 on January 9, 2015. But the stock was not cheap any more. As a disciplined value investor, I did not manage to buy as much as I wanted to in October.

Fast forward to April, CSR price went through another nine days of rapid ascent, with eight out of those nine days seeing price hitting the 10% up limit. I started selling when price hit 20 Yuan, a price that is too dear for the company's growth potential and risk. But a wise investor told me never to sell in a 10% up day. From her decades of experience investing in the A-share market, she concludes that the probability of price going up on the next day is much higher than going down when a stock hits a 10% up limit. "Sell when the limit is broken and trading volume picks up," she suggested.

This trading strategy makes sense because when the 10% price increase limit is hit and there is no trading volume, it suggests there isn't enough ready sellers at the prevailing limit price. Buyers should get ready to pay more. While this logic is not bullet proof, this experience or belief, if you will, can be self-fulfilling. Few would sell in a 10% up day if people believe they could always sell at a higher price on the next day. This crazy 10%-a-day ascent continued until the stock hit 39 on April 20, before it started trading down in an extremely volatile pattern. I sold all those stocks between 35 and 39. So for those losses from not being able to buy as much CSR as I wanted to, I made it back from price overshoots, possibly also thanks to the 10% limit rule.

Lessons learned:

  1. Trade decisively when you identify a good stock. Gradual accumulation only works in a continuous market.
  2. Have an extremely concentrated portfolio can lead to large liquidty risks. Trading halts can last months. This, combined with the 10% price change limit rule, means liquidity risks of A-share stocks are substantially higher than most US stocks.
  3. 10% price change limit rules often lead to price overshoots. Take advantage of it. But be aware of the associated liquidity risk when everyone is trying to sell.
